# Archetypes — 9 structural templates

The linkedin-post-generator skill picks 3 different templates per run, rotated against the previous 2 runs. Each variant in a run uses a different template. The point is structural diversity, so a reader scrolling past three drafts feels like three distinct posts and not three rewrites of one post.

---

## 1. Doctrinal analysis

Open with the rule, case, or doctrine. Walk through the holding in 2-4 sentences with named parties and judges where they're public record. End with the practical implication for an attorney's daily work, then a quiet product tie-in.

> Example: "In United States v. Heppner, Judge Rakoff held that documents a defendant created with a public AI tool... [doctrine]... For internal AI use, the bar is enterprise-grade confidentiality and counsel-directed use. That's the door legal tech needs to walk through."

## 2. Stat opener

Lead with a concrete number or research finding. Unpack what it means in plain language for one line. End with "this is why we built X" or "this is what most legal-tech tools still get wrong."

> Example: "Stanford found that Westlaw's AI hallucinates 33% of the time. Lexis+ AI? 17%. That means 1 in 3 to 1 in 6 citations these tools generate could be completely fake."

## 3. Quoted-question opener

Start with a question Collin keeps getting in literal quote marks. Distinguish his answer from the default playbook. End with a values statement, not a CTA.

> Example: "'Why aren't you charging yet?' Investors ask. Mentors ask. Other founders ask. The standard says charge early. For legal tech, it doesn't apply. We'll charge when we've earned the right to."

## 4. Architectural assertion

Open with a 1-line thesis. Enumerate the components with `→` arrow bullets. Close with what that means for the attorney, framed as a values statement not a feature list.

> Example: "AI is only as capable as the framework around it. → Where does the data come from? → Are citations verified? → Is client data isolated? Strip away the framework and the smartest AI is still just confidently guessing."

## 5. Foil takedown

Lead with a critique of an incumbent (Westlaw, Lexis, Harvey at $11B valuation, etc). State the moat. Reframe the moat as a flaw. End with the simple alternative.

> Example: "Westlaw hasn't meaningfully innovated in 20 years. They don't have to. When you own the data, you don't need a good product. Turns out, the law is public. You just need someone to build a better way to search it."

## 6. Single-paragraph punch

One tight 4-6 sentence block. No bullets. No subsections. No section breaks. Lands like a tweet that grew up. Use this for the Bold variant especially.

> Example: "The law is public. It always has been. Westlaw doesn't own case law. Lexis doesn't own statutes. They own access. That's a different thing, and it's the thing that's about to break."

## 7. Beta-pattern observation

Describe a recurring pattern from beta-tester conversations (anonymized, no specific names, no fabricated quotes). Name what the pattern reveals. Name what was changed because of it.

> Example: "The recurring objection we keep hearing from solo attorneys isn't about price. It isn't about features. It's about the 9 PM new-client-email moment, when they need to spin up a research workflow on a brand-new matter without a junior associate. We rebuilt the matter-creation flow because of that pattern."

## 8. Build-in-public update

A concrete shipped thing from the last week. What was hard about it. What attorneys said when they saw it. Specific, dated, with a real product detail.

> Example: "Shipped the Bluebook citation export this week. The hard part wasn't the formatting. It was matching parallel citations across the Westlaw and OpenLaws conventions without losing the pin cite. First attorney who saw it asked if she could use it on a brief due Friday. That's the test."

## 9. Reframe-the-question

Surface a question lawyers ARE asking. Redirect to the question they SHOULD be asking. Then answer the second one with a real product or doctrine anchor.

> Example: "Most attorneys ask whether AI is accurate enough to use in legal work. The better question: when AI is wrong, can you tell? That's what 'verified' means. Every citation Caseread returns is checked against the source before you ever see it."
